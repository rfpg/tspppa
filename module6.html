Solving intercepts using equations, graphs, and tables involves finding the points where a function or equation intersects the x and y axes. Here's a summary of how to do it using each approach:

Equations:

  • For the x-intercept, set y to zero (y = 0) and solve for the corresponding value(s) of x. This will give you the x-coordinate(s) of the point(s) where the graph crosses the x-axis.
  • For the y-intercept, set x to zero (x = 0) and solve for the corresponding value of y. This will give you the y-coordinate of the point where the graph crosses the y-axis.

Graphs:

  • To find the x-intercept from a graph, locate the point(s) where the graph intersects the x-axis. The x-coordinate(s) of these point(s) represent the x-intercept(s).
  • To find the y-intercept from a graph, locate the point where the graph intersects the y-axis. The y-coordinate of this point represents the y-intercept.

Tables:

  • For the x-intercept, examine the table and find the row(s) where the corresponding y-value is zero (y = 0). The x-values in these rows represent the x-intercept(s).
  • For the y-intercept, locate the row where the x-value is zero (x = 0). The corresponding y-value in this row represents the y-intercept.
  • Solving intercepts is essential for understanding the behavior of functions and their graphical representations. It helps identify key points, such as the roots (zeros) of the equation and the starting point on the y-axis. By utilizing equations, graphs, or tables, mathematicians and scientists can gain valuable insights into various mathematical models and real-world scenarios.

    Example 3: Let us shift our focus now to intercepts from an equation.
    • Determine the intercepts of this line: -5x - 4y = 10:

    Solution:
    • Step 1: Let's start with the x-intercept that would require y to be zero. That would give us -5x = 10.
    • Step 2: Divide both sides by -5 to get the x-intercept: x = -2, so the x-intercept is (-2, 0).
    • Step 3: Now, let's go after the y-intercept that would require x to be zero. -4y = 10.
    • Step 4: Divide both sides by -4 to get the y-intercept. y = -2.5, so the y-intercept is (0, -2.5).
    • Step 5: So, our x and y-intercepts are (-2, 0)(0, -2.5), respectively.



    Example 4: Let's do one more equation for good measure. Determine the intercepts of this line: y = -9x - 14.

    Solution:
    • Step 1: This time, let's start with the y-intercept that would require x to be zero. We immediately get our y-intercept from y = -14 or (0, -14).
    • Step 2: Now let's get the x-intercept that would require y to be zero. This is slightly more involved: 0 = -9x - 14.
    • Step 3: Let's bring -14 over to the left side of the equation and change the sign: 14 = -9x.
    • Step 4: Now, let's divide both sides by -9 to get our x-intercept: 14/-9 = x, which gives us a fraction.
    • Step 5: We can simplify 14/-9 by writing it as -14/9, which yields the same result.
